EndPCA: Ensemble Defense With Provably
Convergent Aggregation Against Poisoning
Attacks in Federated Learning
Mingyue Zhang , Chenyu Hu , Xuelian Cao , Atul Sajjanhar , Senior Member, IEEE, Zheng Yang ,
Muneeb Ul Hassan , Member, IEEE, Zhi Jin , Fellow, IEEE, and Jialong Li , Member, IEEE

Abstract‚ÄĒDespite its success in many applications, federated
learning is increasingly vulnerable to sophisticated poisoning
attacks. Existing defenses, particularly Byzantine Robust Aggregation Rules (BRARs), offer some protection but rely on strong
assumptions or challenging technical prerequisites. To address
these shortcomings, we propose an ensemble defense with provably
convergent aggregation (EndPCA). By using the entropy weight
method to consolidate scores from multiple BRARs into an
ensemble trust score, it effectively integrates heterogeneous weak
BRARs to resist a wide range of poisoning attacks under
practical assumptions. We formally prove that EndPCA can
provide theoretical guarantees of convergence with bounded
error. Our empirical evaluations show that EndPCA consistently
outperforms existing BRARs, demonstrating its effectiveness
across various scenarios.
Index Terms‚ÄĒFederated learning, poisoning attacks, byzantine
robust aggregation rule.

I. I NTRODUCTION
EDERATED learning (FL) enables decentralized client
devices to train machine learning models while adhering
to data privacy constraints. In FL, the server distributes a
global model to clients, which train it locally on private data
and return updates. The server then aggregates these updates to
refine the shared model iteratively until satisfactory accuracy
is achieved. FL offers secure on-device learning and has
been deployed across healthcare [1], finance [2], autonomous
vehicles [3], and Industry 4.0 [4], [5]. It is already used by
companies like Google and Apple in services like Gboard‚Äôs
next-word prediction [6] and Siri [7].

Although FL is valuable, many of its application scenarios,
exemplified by privacy-sensitive sensing, adversarial manipulation and blockchain environments with decentralized trust
and immutable but contamination-prone data [8], [9], [10],
inherently expose models to inference and perturbation risks.
These characteristics suggest that FL systems are increasingly
vulnerable to sophisticated and evolving attacks. This work
focuses on defending against poisoning attacks, which are
considered among the most harmful threats to FL [11], [12],
[13]. Poisoning attacks come in two main types: untargeted
attacks, aiming to reduce the accuracy of the global model
[14], [15], and targeted attacks, aiming to change the model‚Äôs
predictions for specific inputs while leaving others unchanged
[16], [17]. For instance, targeted attacks might involve inserting backdoors [16], [18] into the global model, allowing
adversaries to control predictions for predefined inputs without
triggering suspicion during normal operations.
Byzantine Robust Aggregation Rules (BRARs) are effective against poisoning attacks [19], [20], [21]. BRARs apply
various techniques to analyze the model updates uploaded
by clients, mitigating the negative impact of the poisoned
updates on the global model. We categorize BRARs into
three types: robust statistical-based methods [19], [21], [22],
methods using an auxiliary trusted dataset for evaluation [23],
[24], [25], and hybrid approaches combining both [15], [26],
[27]. They rely on strong (and often impractical) assumptions
or technical prerequisites that are challenging to implement
in practice. Examples include requiring a certain proportion
of honest clients [21], specific properties of honest updates
[28], or a clean and unbiased dataset [26]. Deviations from
these assumptions or technical prerequisites can significantly
undermine their effectiveness. Therefore, although they may
offer robust defenses in specific attack scenarios, they fall
short in defending against a wide range of poisoning attacks,
particularly unknown ones.
We observe that existing BRARs exhibit complementary
strengths: different BRARs excel in specific defensive scenarios, while their weaknesses are often covered by the strengths
of other BRARs. FLTrust [26] performs well in independent
and identically distributed (IID) but struggles in non-IID
settings. FLAME [29] is effective in both, but weakens when
malicious clients exceed half. The key question we address
is whether a unified framework can integrate complementary
BRARs while ensuring provably convergent robust aggregation under diverse and dynamic adversarial conditions.

(EndPCA), a unified theoretical framework that adaptively
integrates multiple base BRARs, each tailored to specific
attack patterns and FL conditions, into a robust and convergent aggregation scheme. Integrating these base BRARs is a
highly technical challenge. Essentially, it involves combining
multiple partial and potentially conflicting assessments of the
same entity into a comprehensive assessment. In particular,
a naive combination (e.g., averaging) of base BRARs often
weakens the overall defense capability. We tackle this challenge by leveraging a core idea of ensemble learning [30],
[31], i.e., multiple weak learners can be combined into a
stronger learner. The design and implementation of EndPCA
incorporate the following key techniques. (1) We develop an
entropy-weight-method-based integration, which calculates the
entropy of the trust scores assigned by BRARs to model
updates and dynamically adjusts an importance score for each
base BRAR. (2) We select a compact yet diverse set of
base BRARs to cover a wide range of poisoning attacks and
FL scenarios, while keeping the system complexity low. To
enable seamless integration, we adapt existing BRARs with
key modifications, such as constructing trusted datasets from
client-side data and using clustering methods to select robust
reference points.
EndPCA not only strengthens protection against poisoning
attacks that the original base BRARs could counter but also
extends defense to attacks they couldn‚Äôt. Importantly, one of
our key design choices is to construct trusted datasets from
client-side data instead of relying on a server-side trusted
dataset. We extend the convergence proof of FLtrust [26]
to this setting, showing that EndPCA achieves convergence
without requiring an auxiliary trusted dataset, which represents
a key innovation not supported by FLtrust. Finally, extensive
experiments demonstrate that EndPCA significantly outperforms strong baselines, including state-of-the-art (SOTA)
BRARs, achieving 0.02% to 7.22% higher accuracy and 0.04%
to 74.02% lower attack success rates.
Contributions. We make the following contributions:
‚ÄĘ We propose a unified defense framework, capable of
robustly integrating heterogeneous weak BRARs, via a
tailored ensemble scoring mechanism based on entropy.
‚ÄĘ We adapt complementary BRARs to instantiate EndPCA,
not by simply combining them, but by bridging their
differences through targeted modifications. These include
constructing client-side trusted datasets and using clustering to select robust references, enabling an efficient and
unified defense across diverse poisoning scenarios.
‚ÄĘ We formally prove that EndPCA can provide theoretical
guarantees of convergence without an auxiliary trusted
dataset, making it more broadly applicable to practical
FL systems than existing BRARs.
‚ÄĘ We empirically evaluate the effectiveness of EndPCA.
The results show that it outperforms existing BRARs
in both metrics related to robustness and backdoor
resilience.

B. Poisoning Attacks
As illustrated in Fig. 1, attackers control malicious clients
to send harmful local model updates to the server with the
intent of corrupting the global model. These malicious updates
may be generated from poisoned local training data [33], [34],
[35] or entirely fabricated [14], [36], [37]. Poisoning attacks
can be classified into untargeted attacks and targeted attacks.
In untargeted attacks, the attacker aims to broadly degrade
the global model‚Äôs accuracy. Examples include the Labelflip
[38], Trim [14], and adaptive attack [14]. Targeted attacks seek
to manipulate the model‚Äôs prediction on specific inputs while
preserving normal behavior elsewhere. A prominent example
of a targeted poisoning attack is the backdoor attack [16], [18],
[36]. Here, the attacker embeds a trigger, such as a unique
pattern or signal, into target test inputs to compromise the
model, forcing it to misclassify or favor specific outcomes.
The typical backdoor attacks are 3DFed [17] and scaling attack
[16].

BRARs defend against poisoning attacks, enabling servers
to learn accurate global models despite Byzantine clients. They

FLDetector [24] excludes outliers through comparing historical updates with the local updates from the current epoch.
In the third methods, FLTrust [26] assigns each client a score
based on the similarity between the model updates trained
on the root dataset and the local model updates. FLest [42]
enhances FLTrust by clustering the clients‚Äô updates. FLCert
[43] integrates multiple BRARs based on the principle of
majority consensus.
However, existing BRARs still face notable limitations.
Firstly, many rely on overly strong assumptions about attackers
and training data. For example, FoolsGold [28] holds diversity
assumption for the honest updates. Krum [22], Bulyan [21],
and Trimmed mean [44] require the proportion of malicious
clients to stay below strict thresholds. Secondly, some BRARs
rely on technical prerequisites that are challenging to implement. For example, FLTrust [26] assumes access to a clean
and unbiased trusted dataset. From the perspective of provable
security, Bulyan [21] and FLCert [43] can guarantee convergence only under strict limits on the number of malicious
clients. Currently, no BRAR can address both targeted and
untargeted attacks under conditions where the trusted
dataset may be biased, or malicious clients may exceed
half, while also providing theoretical guarantees.
III. T HREAT M ODEL AND R EQUIREMENTS
A. Threat Model
We adopt the threat model used in previous works [14], [26],
[45] on poisoning attacks. The attacker‚Äôs goal is to execute
either untargeted or targeted poisoning attacks to undermine
the FL training process. We assume that an attacker can control
at most m malicious clients, where m < n ‚ąí 1, implying that
there is at least one honest client. The attacker can instruct
these malicious clients to execute designated attacking tasks,
including submitting arbitrary model updates to the server.
The server is semi-honest and trusted in computation, while
clients may be malicious but cannot modify other clients‚Äô data
or the server‚Äôs logic. The attacker selects malicious clients
before training but cannot manipulate originally honest clients
during the training process, with client identities (malicious
or honest) remaining unchanged. Meanwhile, the attacker may
exploit its background knowledge of the target FL system to
launch attacks. We focus on the strong poisoning attacks in the
full-knowledge setting [26], [46], which assumes the attacker
knows the global model, the global model updates, the global
learning rate, the server‚Äôs aggregation rule, the loss function,
the number of all clients, the local training data of malicious
clients, and the local model updates on all clients.

B. Design Goals and Requirements
We aim to design a broad-spectrum FL defense that
meets key security requirements: (R1) Defense-integration
robustness: The defense should resist a broad-spectrum of
targeted and untargeted poisoning attacks, preserving the
global model‚Äôs accuracy and backdoor resilience as if no
attacks occurred. (R2) Fidelity: The method should preserve
the global model‚Äôs classification accuracy under no attacks,
matching the performance of FedAvg [32]. (R3) Data distribution independence: The defense should work effectively
for both i.i.d. and non-i.i.d. client data distributions, without
relying on specific distribution characteristics. (R4) Largescale malicious clients resilience: The method must remain
robust against a wide range of malicious client proportions,
performing reliably even when the proportion of honest clients
falls below 10%. (R5) No-Trusted server-side datasets: The
method avoids requiring a trusted dataset on server, relying
solely on client-uploaded information to establish trust and
guide model aggregation.
IV. T HE P ROPOSED E ND PCA
A. Overview of EndPCA
We propose a defense integration method that unifies multiple heterogeneous strategies against diverse poisoning attacks,
while remaining easily extensible. Since naive combinations
(e.g., averaging) often weaken individual defenses, we design
an entropy-weight-method-based integration that merges their
trust scores into a single ensemble trust score to preserve
and enhance their effectiveness. The ensemble score, weighted
by the entropy of defense trust scores (lower entropy contributes more), serves as the aggregation weight in G. The
method also computes a reference model update as the trust
root. We select suitable base BRARs based on the following
principles. (1) Dictatorship, i.e., computing one of the most
‚Äúpromising‚ÄĚ model updates to judge other updates. (2) Diversity, i.e., distinguishing the honest clients and the malicious
ones based on the diversity of their updates. (3) Centroid,
i.e., clustering the model updates to aggregate similar ones
into a ‚Äúhigh-promising‚ÄĚ updates. (4) Trigger detection, i.e.,
identifying changes in specific neurons to determine if a
backdoor is implanted. While no single principle meets all five
requirements.

A. Experimental Setup
1) Comparison BRARs and Attacks: We provide extensive
experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of EndPCA, by
comparing the accuracy of the learned global model and attack
success rate with FedAvg [32] and several comparison BRARs,
including FLTrust [26], FLest [42], Foolsgold [28], FLDetector
[24], FLAME [29], RFLBAT [39], Deepsight [19], and BackdoorIndicator [40]. We consider several poisoning attacks:
Labelflip (LF) [38], Updateflip (UF) [15], Trim [14], Adaptive
attack [14], Scaling attack (SA) [16], 3DFed [17], ALIE [52],
IPM [53], and DistanceMaximization [54]. The settings for
LF, UF, Trim and Adaptive follow that in the FLTrust [26],

while SA and 3DFed use configurations from their original
papers [16], [17]. ALIE, IPM and DistanceMaximization are

2) Benchmark: Following the experimental setup in the
previous work [17], [20], [23], [24], [26], [55], we evaluate
our method on the following datasets: MNIST [56], FashionMNIST [57], CIFAR10 [58], Tiny-Imagenet [59], and HAR

effectiveness of attack and defense methods. These datasets
cover a range of dimensions, including lightweight to largescale datasets, small size to large size, grayscale to color
images, and specific object classification to general object
classification. The neural network architectures include MLP,
CNNs FCNs, and Resnet18. To simulate the IID and non-IID
datasets, we employ the label distribution [61]. The implementation aligns with the method described in [17], [26], and

a Dirichlet-based label-partitioning scheme. For each class,
we draw a length-n probability vector (p1 , . . . , pn ) ‚ąľ Dir(őĪ)
and assign the class‚Äôs samples to the n clients proportionally
according to these probabilities. This procedure is repeated
independently for all classes, yielding a heterogeneous dataset
whose class distributions differ across clients. We set the
Dirichlet parameter as őĪ = b, where b denotes the bias
probability: values of b close to 1 produce more uniform
(near-IID) allocations, whereas values close to 0 yield highly
skewed, strongly non-IID partitions. Other FL settings align
with those in the previous works [17], [26].
3) Metrics: We evaluate robustness and fidelity using model
accuracy (ACC), which is the ratio of correctly classified test
inputs. The backdoor resilience is evaluated through attack
success rate (ASR), which is the ratio of attacker-targeted
inputs misclassified as the target label. Metrics are computed
with a test batch size of 100. Higher ACC indicates greater
robustness, lower ASR reflects stronger backdoor resilience,
and fidelity is measured by how closely the ACC matches
FedAvg in non-attack scenarios.
Summary of Experimental Results. We highlight the
following conclusions regarding security requirements.
Defense-integration robustness: EndPCA shows the highest
robustness and significantly reduces backdoor attack success
rates. Fidelity: EndPCA achieves model accuracy comparable
to FedAvg. Data distribution independence: EndPCA
maintains robustness and backdoor resilience under both
IID and non-IID datasets. Large-scale malicious clients
resilience: EndPCA consistently delivers strong robustness
and backdoor resilience across varying malicious client
proportions. Moreover, EndPCA does not rely on any
auxiliary trusted dataset, satisfying security requirement R5.

variants of EndPCA are considered. The five variants are:
w/o DIC, w/o DIV, w/o CEN, and w/o TRD, which are

VI. C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
We developed EndPCA, a broad-spectrum defense against
diverse poisoning attacks. Our theoretical analysis proves convergence with bounded error, and experiments show superior
performance across datasets, neural networks, and attack settings. This work demonstrates that integrating heterogeneous
weak BRARs through an entropy-based dynamic weighting
scheme can achieve not only enhanced empirical robustness

against diverse poisoning attacks, but also provable convergence under mild assumptions without requiring a trusted
dataset. This addresses a fundamental limitation of prior
defenses by unifying practical effectiveness with theoretical
guarantees in federated learning.
There remain several natural avenues for extension. While
EndPCA integrates BRARs effectively, it has yet to incorporate other defenses, such as knowledge distillation [63],
adversarial training [64], and moving target defense [65]. As
for applicability, future work may extend EndPCA to smartmeter networks, ISAC sensing systems, and cyber-physical
power infrastructures, where adversarial behaviors may be
multimodal, coordinated, or trigger-free [5], [8], [9], [10].
These settings introduce new threat patterns, and adapting the
modular scoring framework of EndPCA to such environments
is a natural direction for future research. Our theoretical guarantees rely on strong convexity and Lipschitz smoothness to
enable a tractable analysis. Extending the convergence analysis
of EndPCA to weaker or non-smooth conditions, particularly
those reflecting the non-convexity of deep neural networks in
practical FL, is an important direction for future work. Such
extensions would further enhance the relevance of our theoretical insights to real-world deployments. Another promising
direction is to reduce the computational cost of Hessian-based
trigger detection, for example by using lightweight curvature
approximations, Hessian-free techniques, or alternative triggerdetection mechanisms [66] that avoid explicit second-order
computations. Another avenue is to analyze entropy-weighting
under adaptive adversaries through a game-theoretic lens,
which may help characterize bounded manipulation and further strengthen robustness guarantees.
